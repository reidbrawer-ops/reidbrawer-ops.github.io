Learn

Never played? Here's everything you need in about two minutes.

The rules are simple enough to learn courtside. The part that actually intimidates first-timers is social — not knowing the local routine for getting on a court. This page covers both, so you can show up to any city on this site and not feel lost.

0 prior experience needed ~2 min read

The three rules that actually matter

Everything else in pickleball is a variation on these. Learn these three and you'll follow any game you watch.

Scoring

Games are usually played to 11, win by 2. Only the serving side can score a point. In doubles, you'll hear the score called as three numbers — your score, the opponent's score, and a "1" or "2" for which partner is serving. That third number confuses everyone at first; don't worry about tracking it yourself, your side will call it.

The serve & two-bounce rule

Serves are underhand only, hit diagonally cross-court, with at least one foot behind the baseline. After the serve, the ball has to bounce once on the return before it can be hit out of the air, and then bounce once more on the serving side before anyone volleys — the "two-bounce rule." After those two bounces, either side can start volleying.

The kitchen

The "kitchen" is the non-volley zone: a 7-foot strip on each side of the net. You can't hit the ball out of the air while standing in it — you can only step in to hit a ball that's already bounced, then step back out. Most beginner mistakes happen here, and most players will just call it and move on, not make it a thing.

Paddle-stack: how you actually get on a court

Most public courts on this site are drop-in, first-come-first-served, with no reservation system. Here's the unwritten routine.

  • When every court is full, look for a line of paddles resting against the fence or stood up at the net — that's the queue. Add yours to the end of the line.
  • Groups of four paddles get called up together for a doubles game as a court opens up. If you're playing solo, ask the group ahead of you if you can join in — this is completely normal and expected.
  • What happens after a game varies by court: some run "winners stay, losers rotate to the back of the line," others rotate everyone off so the queue keeps moving on busy days. Watch one game before you play, or just ask — nobody minds explaining it.
  • If you don't see a paddle line at all, the court is probably quiet enough that you can just ask whoever's playing how they're rotating people in.

What to bring your first time

You don't need much, and you don't need it to be nice.

  • A paddle — if you don't have one, ask at the rec department or clubhouse before you assume you need to buy something; some public sites keep a small stash of loaners or know a member willing to lend one for a game.
  • Court or tennis shoes — flat, non-marking soles with lateral support. Running shoes are built for forward motion only and are a common way to roll an ankle on a hard court.
  • Water and sun protection — almost every court on this site is outdoors and unshaded. Bring more water than you think you need.
  • A layer for wind or fog — coastal and bayfront courts on this site run noticeably cooler and windier than inland ones; check a city's page for its weather rating before you decide what to wear.
  • Outdoor balls, if you're bringing your own — outdoor pickleballs have smaller, more numerous holes than indoor ones and hold up better on asphalt or concrete. Most drop-in groups will have extras; you don't need to show up with your own.

Before you go

The fear that keeps most beginners from showing up is social, not logistical — worrying about being watched or slowing a game down. In practice, most drop-in groups have an unspoken patience for anyone visibly new: missed shots happen at every skill level, and stacking your paddle is normally all it takes for someone to walk you through the local routine in the first thirty seconds. The rules above are more than most first-timers arrive knowing.
