Learn to play

Never played before? Start here.

Four things to know before your first game — everything else you'll pick up on court.

01 · Scoring

Games go to 11, win by 2.

Only the serving side can score a point. In doubles, the score is called as three numbers: your score, their score, then your server number — 1 or 2.

02 · The kitchen

The 7-foot no-volley zone.

You can't hit the ball out of the air while standing inside the kitchen. Step in only to play a ball that has already bounced, then step back out.

03 · The serve

Underhand, below the waist.

Serve diagonally, clearing the kitchen. Both the serve and the return must bounce once before anyone can volley — the "two-bounce rule."

04 · Court etiquette

Call your own lines, share the court.

Line calls are on the honor system — call your own out balls. On a busy court, rotate off after 15–20 minutes if others are waiting.

Paddle-stack: how you actually get on a court

Most public courts on this site are drop-in, first-come-first-served, with no reservation system. Here's the unwritten routine.

  • When every court is full, look for a line of paddles resting against the fence or stood up at the net — that's the queue. Add yours to the end.
  • Groups of four paddles get called up together for doubles as a court opens. Playing solo? Ask the group ahead if you can join in — completely normal.
  • What happens after a game varies by court: some run "winners stay, losers rotate to the back," others rotate everyone off to keep the queue moving. Watch one game before you play, or just ask.
  • No paddle line at all? The court's probably quiet enough to just ask whoever's playing how they're rotating people in.

What to bring your first time

You don't need much, and you don't need it to be nice.

  • A paddle — ask at the rec department or clubhouse before assuming you need to buy one; some public sites keep a stash of loaners or know a member willing to lend one.
  • Court or tennis shoes — flat, non-marking soles with lateral support. Running shoes are built for forward motion only and are a common way to roll an ankle.
  • Water and sun protection — almost every court on this site is outdoors and unshaded.
  • A layer for wind or fog — coastal and bayfront courts run noticeably cooler and windier than inland ones; check a city's weather rating before you dress.
  • Outdoor balls, if you're bringing your own — smaller, more numerous holes than indoor balls, and they hold up better on asphalt or concrete. Most drop-in groups have extras.

Ready for your own paddle? No rush — but when you are, take the 2-minute quiz to match your game and budget.

Before you go

Most drop-in groups have an unspoken patience for anyone visibly new — missed shots happen at every skill level, and stacking your paddle is usually all it takes for someone to walk you through the local routine. You already know more than most first-timers arrive knowing.
