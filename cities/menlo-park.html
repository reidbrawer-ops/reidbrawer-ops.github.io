Menlo Park

Menlo Park's public scene runs through two neighborhood parks, not one flagship: Kelly Park, where half the courts can be reserved ahead of time, and Nealon Park, pure drop-in and reportedly the busier of the two. Eight real courts between them. Burgess Park — the site the city talked about converting back in 2023 — still isn't one of them.

Courts #1–2 can be reserved in advance ($10/hr residents, $15/hr non-residents for recreational play; $25/hr for commercial use or instruction); courts #3–4 are drop-in only. City policy guarantees at least half of every court site stays first-come-first-served, so there's always a walk-on option even during someone else's reservation window.

All four courts are walk-on only — there's no way to reserve here, which is exactly why it's reportedly the more popular of Menlo Park's two parks. Good option if you want casual, beginner-leaning morning play and don't mind a wait at peak times.

What happened to Burgess Park

Burgess Park (425 Burgess Drive) still has no pickleball — just two tennis courts. A 2023 proposal to convert its basketball court into up to three pickleball areas stalled after commissioners objected to losing a court that gets heavy use from local kids and teens. A separate, higher-priority plan for two new dedicated courts at Willow Oaks Park was floated in a March 2024 council discussion, estimated at 12–36 months out — as of this research, Willow Oaks Park's own city page still describes only soccer, tennis, and a dog area, with no pickleball. Neither project has broken ground.

  • Pickleball paddles and balls are available to borrow for free from the Menlo Park Library — useful if you're visiting without gear.
  • If both parks are full, Mitchell Park in Palo Alto (15 courts) and Red Morton Park in Redwood City (4 courts) are the closest dedicated alternatives.
  • Court counts and reservation splits are set by city policy and can shift — check the city's page below before planning around a specific court.
